It may be that Exercise 1 will be too demanding for many introductory students (keeping track of multiple variables, and making a contour plot). It may be more effective to skip Exercise 1 and start with the case where the bars are all identical. The case of identical bars can be motivated because simulation results can be compared to experimental data obtained using three identical meter sticks with two large binder clips, a pencil with a round cross section to serve as a rotation axis, and a stopwatch. By having different groups of students use different overlaps, it is possible to obtain a data set that spans the range of scaled overlaps from 0 to 1 (provided you have a lot of meter sticks handy) within a class period.

Depending on instructor preference, a working version of the computational model required for these exercises could be directly provided to the student; or the student could be required to modify, or add to, a nearly completed computational model; or the student could be required to build the model from scratch; or really anything in between these scenarios. Another possibility for producing a working model, time permitting, is to build it together with the students in class. To carry out the exercises the student must have access to either a plotting program, or use a programming environment with built-in graphics capabilities. In the exercises, different configurations of the physical pendulum are suggested for analysis. Additionally, a quadratic model of drag is included (Exercise 3 and 4)
