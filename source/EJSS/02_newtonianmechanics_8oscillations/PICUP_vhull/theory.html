The simplest example of Simple Harmonic Oscillation (SHO) is a mass hanging on a spring. In equilibrium, the string will have stretched some equilibrium distance xoxo, so that the weight of the mass mgmg is balanced by the spring force −kxo−kxo. Any additional motion xx of the spring will result in an unbalanced force:

ΣF=ma=mg−k(xo+x)(1)(1)ΣF=ma=mg−k(xo+x)
md2xdt2=(mg−kxo)−kx(2)(2)md2xdt2=(mg−kxo)−kx
d2xdt2=−kmx(3)(3)d2xdt2=−kmx

This last equation is in the form of the equation for SHO:

d2xdt2=−ω2x(4)(4)d2xdt2=−ω2x

where ω≡km−−√ω≡km in this case. Knowing ωω, we can then go on to do whatever we need to do with the problem. The period of the motion is T=2πωT=2πω, the position as a function of time will be x(t)=Acos(ωt+ϕ)x(t)=Acos⁡(ωt+ϕ), etc.

We can use this “known solution” to SHO any time we can get the equation of motion into the form of the SHO differential equation. If the second derivative of a variable is equal to a negative constant times the variable, the solution is SHO with ωω equal to the square root of that constant.

Even when we can’t get the equation of motion for a system into the form of SHO, we can often approximate the motion as SHO for small oscillations. The classic example of this is the simple pendulum. The equation of motion for the pendulum is

d2θdt2=−gLsinθ(5)(5)d2θdt2=−gLsin⁡θ

This equation is not SHO, but for small θθ, sinθ≈θsin⁡θ≈θ so we can approximate the motion as SHO as long as the oscillations are small.

In the case of our wedge-shaped boat, the equation of motion is

d2γdt2=−ω2o(1+γ2)γ(6)(6)d2γdt2=−ωo2(1+γ2)γ

This is not SHO! If γγ is small, it is approximately SHO, but only approximately.
