Exercise 2: A V-hulled boat

The derivation for the V-hulled boat follows the same general procedure.

V=12βh2L=12βL(yo+y)2(16)(16)V=12βh2L=12βL(yo+y)2
ΣF=md2ydt2=mg−12ρgβL(y2o+2yoy+y2)(17)(17)ΣF=md2ydt2=mg−12ρgβL(yo2+2yoy+y2)

The equilibrium position is given by yoyo, so mg−12ρgβLy2o=0mg−12ρgβLyo2=0 and

d2ydt2=−ρgβL2m(2yoy+y2)(18)(18)d2ydt2=−ρgβL2m(2yoy+y2)

This can be further simplified as

d2ydt2=−ρgβLyom(1+y2yo)y(19)(19)d2ydt2=−ρgβLyom(1+y2yo)y

From that equation we can see that if y2yoy2yo is small, then the motion is approximately SHO with

ωo=ρgβLyom−−−−−−−√(20)(20)ωo=ρgβLyom

There are an awful lot of constants in that equation, so to simplify our numeric calculations let’s just redefine things.

d2ydt2=−ω2o(1+y2yo)y(21)(21)d2ydt2=−ωo2(1+y2yo)y

We can also define the unitless variable γ≡yyoγ≡yyo, which allows us to rewrite the equation in (almost) unit-independent form:

d2γdt2=−ω2o(1+γ2)γ(22)(22)d2γdt2=−ωo2(1+γ2)γ

That’s the equation we need to send through the ODE solver.

The equation is approximately SHO if the amplitude is small. The easist way to control the amplitude is to set initial position to yiyi, with vi=0vi=0, so that the amplitude is yiyi.

Setting different values of amplitude gives different behavior: for small amplitude (γo=0.1γo=0.1) the result is nearly indistinguishable from SHO, but for larger values both the period and symmetry change. There’s an opportunity here for the model to break down. If the boat gets completely out of the water, then the width of the boat becomes negative (ok, it doesn’t really but it does in the model) so then the volume becomes negative and the buoyant force becomes negative and the boat leaps away from the water exactly the way that real boats don’t. Be prepared to discuss this interesting result should the students get the amplitude too high.
