Euler Method

One computational approach is based on making a sound approximation to the time-dependent acceleration. If tt is sufficiently small, then ay(t)ay(t) will be approximately equal to its average value over the time interval tt : ay(t)≈ay,avg=ΔvyΔtay(t)≈ay,avg=ΔvyΔt. Thus,

ay(t)≈vy(t+Δt)−vy(t)Δt.(4)(4)ay(t)≈vy(t+Δt)−vy(t)Δt.

Solving Equation 4 for vy(t+Δt)vy(t+Δt), we arrive at

vy(t+Δt)≈vy(t)+ay(t)Δt.(5)(5)vy(t+Δt)≈vy(t)+ay(t)Δt.

In a similar manner, if we approximate vy(t)vy(t) by its average value over a small time interval tt then the following equation results for the position at time t+Δtt+Δt:

y(t+Δt)≈y(t)+vy(t)Δt.(6)(6)y(t+Δt)≈y(t)+vy(t)Δt.

This particular computational strategy is known as the Euler method. Equations 5 and 6 are called finite difference equations, and can be used to predict the values of the velocity and position at a later time t+Δtt+Δt if their respective values, and acceleration, are known at the previous time tt. As long as tt is sufficiently small, then these equations can be iterated a sufficient number of times to produce accurate predictions for the velocity and position at any future time.

For our specific consideration of the hanging SHO, we replace ay(t)ay(t) in Equation 5 with the expression in Equation 2, obtained by applying Newton’s 2nd Law. Thus,

vy(t+Δt)≈vy(t)−kmy(t)Δt.(7)(7)vy(t+Δt)≈vy(t)−kmy(t)Δt.

Equations 6 and 7 constitute the Euler scheme for iteratively solving the hanging SHO. If we know the velocity and position of the mass mm at time t=0t=0, then Equations 6 and 7 can be applied to predict their respective values at the later time t+dtt+dt. We then use the values of velocity and position at time t+dtt+dt to predict those at time t+2dtt+2dt by again applying Equations 6 and 7. This process is repeated for a sufficient number of times (usually a very large number!) to attain predictions for the velocity and position at the desired future time tt.

At this point it is convenient to change the notation from the explicit time dependence to an index-based format. Let ti=itti=it, where ii is an integer known as the time index. i=0i=0 corresponds to t=0t=0, i=1i=1 to t=tt=t, i=2i=2 to t=2tt=2t, and so on. Euler-Cromer Method

Another computational strategy is known as the modified Euler method, or the Euler-Cromer method. The Euler-Cromer algorithm can be obtained through a minor modification of the simpler Euler algorithm. Instead of using the velocity at the beginning of the time interval vi−1vi−1 to iteratively update the position, as in Equation 10, the value of the velocity at the end of the time interval vivi is used to update the position for each time step.
