The parameters used for these plots were:g=9.8 ms2, k=10 Nm, m=1 kg, y0=0.1 m, v0=0,Δt=0.01 sg=9.8 ms2, k=10 Nm, m=1 kg, y0=0.1 m, v0=0,Δt=0.01 s. The mass oscillates about the equilibrium position, which is 0 on the position graph. Obviously the Euler method doesn’t work as there is a growing amplitude in both, the position and velocity. Interestingly, the correct period of oscillation is produced by the Euler algorithm.

In attempting to get rid of the growing amplitude, a student may triumphantly claim success if a very small value of ΔtΔt is used. A value of Δt=0.001 sΔt=0.001 s and smaller (used in tandem with the above values for the parameters) can actually make the Euler solution and the exact solution nearly identical for a few oscillations. If this scenario is observed by the students, they should be encouraged to take the simulation out further in time at least to the point where a significant deviation of the Euler solution from the exact one is again observed, reinforcing the point that making ΔtΔt smaller only masks the problem, but doesn’t fundamentally get rid of the instability.

Exercise 3: Energy in the Euler Algorithm Model of a SHO

The energy vs. time graph below, generated with the same physical parameters as those used in the solutions for Exercises 1 and 2 above, but with two different time steps, sheds some light on the problem of the observed artifacts in the position and velocity amplitude behavior: energy is not conserved in the Euler algorithm, and increases faster than linear in time. The increase in energy can be reduced by making ΔtΔt smaller, as shown in the graph, but cannot be made identically equal to zero.

Exercise 5: Energy in the Euler-Cromer Algorithm Model of a SHO

The graph below shows the energy as a function of time for the Euler-Cromer algorithm, using the same parameters as those used in the sample solution for exercises 1 and 2 above.

Interestingly, energy is not actually conserved for the Euler-Cromer algorithm, but oscillates with a very small amplitude about the value of the exact constant energy of the system. The oscillations would not be observed if the graph were produced at a larger range in values on the energy axis, and energy would thus appear to be time-independent. The oscillation amplitude can be made smaller with smaller ΔtΔt, but not eradicated. It is an interesting exercise to directly calculate the energy difference between two successive generic time steps by substituting the Euler-Cromer equations into the expression for the total energy. The resulting expression is informative but I’ve found the analysis to be a bit too advanced for introductory students.
