For this Exercise Set, a mass suspended from a vertical, hanging spring was chosen for the SHO, rather than a horizontally oriented SHO that does not include gravity, as the results could accompany an experiment done in an introductory mechanics lab. The analysis, required for the vertical SHO, although slightly more complicated than the horizontal problem, is appropriate for introductory students.

Exercise 1 asks the students to build the computational model of the hanging spring-mass system. Depending on instructor preference, a working version of the computational model could be directly provided to the student; or the student could be required to modify, or add to, a nearly completed computational model; or the student required to build the model from scratch; or really anything in between these scenarios. Another possibility for producing a working model, time permitting, is to build it, or parts of it, together with the students in class. To carry out the subsequent exercises the student must have the working program, and also access to either a plotting program, or a programming environment with built-in graphics capabilities.

It might be best for students to have produced, as a prerequisite to this exercise set, a computational model or pseudocode algorithm, of a simple non-oscillatory system, utilizing the Euler method. Having gone through, for instance, the Falling Sphere with Air Resistance Proportional to v2v2 exercise set in the PICUP collection, the students should be well familiar with the implementation of the Euler scheme; and, furthermore, will likely approach this SHO system with some confidence, expecting the Euler algorithm to work marvelously.

It can be an entertaining experience (for faculty), and a learning-enhancing one (for students), to take students through the exercise of observing their precious Euler method fail miserably, and of trying to eradicate the growing amplitude that emerges (Exercises 1 and 2) when solving the SHO with the Euler algorithm. If the students have already been exposed to computational exercises utilizing the Euler method to solve non-oscillatory systems, then they will likely attempt to get rid of the growing amplitude artifact by systematically reducing ΔtΔt. While reducing ΔtΔt does reduce the rate at which the amplitude in position and velocity grows, the amplitude still grows without bound (Exercise 2). By not letting my students in right away on the secret that the Euler algorithm doesn’t work for oscillatory systems, I try to guide them in coming to the realization that something else may sometimes be needed to produce an accurate model beyond merely making ΔtΔtsufficiently small. This exercise set can serve as a first opportunity to drive home the importance of addressing the dual challenges of computational accuracy and algorithmic stability.

Of course, this simple SHO system can be solved analytically without difficulty; but, the computational approach, wherein students discover the limitations of the Euler method, and gain experience with the modified Euler algorithm in the context of testing accuracy and stability with the aid of a known exact solution, provides educational value, as well as acts as a springboard for taking on more complex and nonlinear oscillatory systems. For example, a next step for the burgeoning computational-minded student would be to tackle the Damped Oscillations, the Damped Driven SHO, or perhaps the Plane Rigid Pendulum in the PICUP collection.
